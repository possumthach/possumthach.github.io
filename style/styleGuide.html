Grammatical conventions: Spelling

In general, IHIE uses American English spelling conventions, with some exceptions:

  • Judgement vs judgment: use judgement.
  • Grey vs gray: use grey.

Grammatical conventions: Text Formatting

  • Bold, italic, and underline styles include connected punctuation, except for closing parentheses and quotation marks.

Grammatical conventions: Punctuation

  • Quotation marks always include connected punctuation.
    • What do you mean, "predominantly?"
    • Under "normal circumstances," yeah, but we're currently in a sort of "abnormal circumstance."
  • If the first character within a quotation is an apostrophe, insert a space between it and the first quotation mark.
    • " 'Sup," he says.
  • Double dashes are used to indicate stops or interruptions in speech. When an interruption happens midsentence, text resumes without restarting initial capitalization. Midsentence dashes are "floated" with spaces on either side.
    • I don't -- sorry, what did you just say?
    If the interruption is the last part of the message, ending punctuation that indicates tone is placed at the end of the dash.
    • Hey, where are you --!
    Otherwise, it is left out.
    • Hold on a second --
    If the interruption occurs between words, the dash is preceded by a space. If the interruption is mid-word, the dash connects to the last letter of the utterance.
    • I don't underst--
  • Ellipses are used to indicate hesitation or less abrupt pauses than double dashes. Ellipses are usually three dots, unless they are extended for comedic effect, at which point they can be arbitrarily long.
    • Just kidding. Unless.............
    Ellipses at the start of a sentence do not connect to the next character and do not interrupt initial capitalization.
    • ... Is that all?
    Midsentence ellipses are connected to the preceding word and spaced from the following word. The sentence resumes without restarting initial capitalization.
    • You don't... know what that is?
  • Exclamation points and question marks are usually used singularly, but can be multiplied to express extreme emotions. No more than two should be used, except for comedic effect.
    • This is the best day of my life!!
    • She said what??
    • You suck!!!!!!
    Exclamation points and question marks can be used at the same time, and are always written as ?! when used in this way.
    • What the fuck are you talking about?!
  • Serial commas are used for lists of three or more things.
    • Mean lady, dog boy, sunshine girl, and the other one.
  • Parenthetical statements at the end of sentences should be merged into the rest of the sentence whenever possible. If not, ending punctuation goes inside the parentheses.
    • Nothing has changed (but not everything has stayed the same.)
... But all of this can be ignored for the sake of keeping dialogue snappy and interesting. The priorities of character dialogue are:
  1. Communicates information clearly and coherently
    • * Human beings are not always perfectly unambiguous when talking! If something IS unclear, it should seem intentional, not like a failure of the author; disambiguate with follow-up statements, or have characters remark that they don't know how to interpret something.
  2. Sounds natural -- flow, rhythm, and diction are consistent with the speaker's character traits, word choice is appropriately varied
  3. Gives opportunity for other characters to respond and continue the conversation
    • * Conversations should come to a natural stopping point, minimizing low-information "tails" like that's enough or we need to get going. If characters run out of things to talk about, they can just stop talking!
  4. Perfect grammar and style are aaaall the way down here. The only times grammatical and stylistic guidelines should overtake precepts 1, 2, and 3 are if "strict adherence to the rules" is itself part of precept 2! Think: would this character interrupt their natural speech pattern to rephrase something in a more "grammatically correct" way? Would they correct someone else, or comment on weird phrasing? How aware are they of these rules in the first place?
Some characters have their own internally consistent grammar rules that differ slightly from the universal ones. See the "Character Writing" sections for specifics.
