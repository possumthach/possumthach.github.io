Again: this is not news to anyone. We all know that English spelling rules are inconsistent and there are plenty of words you would never be able to guess the pronunciation of based only on how they're written, nor the spelling based only on how they're said. But even for the gnarliest English words like "phlegm" and "cnidocyte", you could at least approximate them phonetically -- if you were taking quick notes in your marine biology lecture and didn't have time to check how those words were supposed to be spelled, you could write down "flem" and "naidosite" and still get the general idea. If you grew up speaking American English, you know exactly what this means -- it means "I don't know", of course. But how would you describe the sound of this word? It's not really "letter sounds", it's three notes, made by humming one continuous sound that shifts in pitch from low to high to low again. There's a little bit of creakiness in there, too. If you make the same "wave" of pitch with your highest, headiest, falsetto-est voice, it doesn't really sound like the same thing, does it?

This is the point where the skeptics in the audience are politely raising their hands to say "hey, this isn't a word at all!" Counterpoint: why not? Can you come up with a sensible definition of "word" that includes every other part of spoken English except this one? If so, the field of linguistics would love to hear it. Write a paper. While you're doing that, the rest of us are going to take it as a given that this auditory language unit is in fact a "word" and move on.

WHAT IT'S NOT

I want to make it clear that the word I'm talking about is not "iunno" or "iono". Those are like, a midpoint on the spectrum from fully-enunciated "I don't know" to this unspellable sound. When you say "iono", you are actually opening your mouth and saying most of the sounds in "I don't know", only dropping the /d/ and /t/, which is the same sort of thing that happens when "going to" becomes "gonna" and "want to" becomes "wanna". The word that is the subject of this article is distinct from "iunno", and I'm gonna need you to recognize that in order to move forward with the discussion.
